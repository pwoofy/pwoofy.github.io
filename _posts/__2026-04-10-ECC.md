<h2>How ECC Actually Works</h2>

Before we break the challenge, let's look at what an Elliptic Curve actually is. The standard Weierstrass equation for a curve looks like this:
$$y^2 \equiv x^3 + Ax + B \pmod p$$

You might be wondering: **Why this specific equation?** Because this equation creates a very specific geometric shape. If you draw a straight line through any two points on this curve, that line is mathematically guaranteed to intersect the curve at exactly one other point. 

This is the basic idea of what point addition is. If you start at a point $G$, you can shoot a line to bounce off the curve to a new point ($2G$). Then bounce again to $3G$, $4G$, and so on. 

<details>
  <summary><b>💡 Interactive: How does a computer "draw a line"? (Click to reveal)</b></summary>
  Computers don't have rulers. To "draw a line" between two points, the computer has to calculate the algebraic slope of that line ($m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$). <br><br>

  Let's say we want to find the slope between two points on our curve: $(5, 1)$ and $(10, 6)$. <br>
  $$m = \frac{6 - 1}{10 - 5} = \frac{5}{5} = 1$$
  
  Once the computer has the slope, it plugs it into a simple algebra formula to find the exact $X$ and $Y$ coordinates of the next "bounce". <br><br>

Because the computer is just doing basic addition and multiplication, it can calculate millions of bounces in a fraction of a second. But if I just show you the final coordinate, it is mathematically impossible for you to work backward and figure out how many times it bounced. This street is the principle of <b>Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP)</b>.
</details>

<br>
<h3>The Golden Rule: Why must $p$ be a Prime Number?</h3>

Look closely at the slope formula we just used: $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$. 

Notice the division? In normal math, division is easy. But remember, ECC uses modulo. In modulo math, there are no decimals. You can't just have $0.5$. So how do you divide?

In modulo math, **division means finding a modular inverse.** <br>
Asking "What is $1 \div 2 \pmod{17}$?" is the same as asking: *"What number can I multiply by 2 to get a remainder of 1?"*

Let's test it:
$2 \times 9 = 18$. 
$18 \pmod{17}$ leaves a remainder of **1**. 
Therefore, in Modulo 17, $1 \div 2 = 9$.

<details>
  <summary><b>⚠️ The Trap: What happens if $p$ is NOT prime? (Click to reveal)</b></summary>
  Let's see what happens if a CTF author uses a composite number, like $p = 10$. <br><br>
  
  Your computer is trying to bounce a point, and it needs to calculate $1 \div 2 \pmod{10}$.<br>
  It asks the question: <i>"What number can I multiply by 2 to get a remainder of 1 in Modulo 10?"</i><br><br>
  
  Let's try every possible number:<br>
  $2 \times 1 = 2$<br>
  $2 \times 2 = 4$<br>
  $2 \times 3 = 6$<br>
  $2 \times 4 = 8$<br>
  $2 \times 5 = 10 \equiv 0 \pmod{10}$<br>
  $2 \times 6 = 12 \equiv 2 \pmod{10}$<br><br>

  Do you see the problem? Multiplying by 2 in Modulo 10 only ever outputs even numbers! It will <b>never</b> hit 1. Because there is no answer, the number 2 has no inverse. The division is mathematically impossible.

  Using a strictly prime number for $p$ guarantees that every single number on the grid can be cleanly divided, keeping the point to be able to bounce forever.
</details>

<details>
  <summary><b>💡 Interactive: A Tiny Example (Click to reveal)</b></summary>
  Let's shrink everything down. Let's say our prime is $p = 17$, $A = 2$, and $B = 2$. <br>
  Our tiny curve equation is:
  $$y^2 \equiv x^3 + 2x + 2 \pmod{17}$$

  Let's check if the point $(5, 1)$ is on our curve! We plug in $x = 5$ and $y = 1$:
  <br><br>
  <b>The Left Side ($y^2$):</b><br>
  $1^2 = 1$<br><br>
  
  <b>The Right Side ($x^3 + 2x + 2$):</b><br>
  $5^3 + 2(5) + 2$<br>
  $125 + 10 + 2 = 137$<br><br>

  Now, we apply the modulo: $137 \pmod{17}$. <br>
  Since $17 \times 8 = 136$, the remainder is exactly <b>1</b>.<br><br>

  Because $1 \equiv 1 \pmod{17}$, the math balances perfectly! The point $(5, 1)$ legally exists on this curve. We will use this as our Generator point $G$.
</details>

<h3>How do we actually encrypt a PIN?</h3>

Unlike RSA, ECC isn't typically used to encrypt messages directly. Instead, it is used to securely agree on a shared secret across a public channel (like a Discord chat). This is called **Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH)**, which is exactly what our CTF challenge uses!

<details>
  <summary><b>💡 Interactive: Encrypting a PIN with ECDH (Click to reveal)</b></summary>
  Let's say Alice wants to send the secret PIN code <b>99</b> to Bob. They are using our tiny Modulo 17 curve with Generator $G = (5, 1)$.<br><br>

  <b>1. Alice generates her keys</b><br>
  Alice secretly picks a random number of "bounces". Let's say her private key is $d_A = 2$. <br>
  She bounces the point $G$ two times. The math outputs a new coordinate: $Q = (6, 3)$. <br>
  She shouts $Q$ across the internet to Bob.<br><br>

  <b>2. Bob generates his keys</b><br>
  Bob secretly picks his own number of bounces. His private key is $d_B = 10$. <br>
  He bounces $G$ ten times, landing on $R = (7, 11)$. <br>
  He shouts $R$ across the internet to Alice.<br><br>

  <b>3. The Magic (The Shared Secret)</b><br>
  So, what actually *is* a "bounce"? <br><br>

  In ECC, bouncing a point means adding it to itself. If you start at $G$ and bounce once, you get $2G$. Bounce again, you get $3G$. <br>
  When Alice picked her private key $d_A = 2$, she calculated $2 \times G$. The result was $Q = (6, 3)$.<br>
  When Bob picked his private key $d_B = 10$, he calculated $10 \times G$. The result was $R = (7, 11)$.<br><br>
  
  Now, look at what happens when they exchange those public points:<br><br>

  Alice takes Bob's point $R$ (which is already 10 bounces) and bounces it by her secret (2). <br>
  Mathematically, she is doing: $2 \times (10 \times G) = 20 \times G$.<br><br>

  Bob takes Alice's point $Q$ (which is already 2 bounces) and bounces it by his secret (10).<br>
  Mathematically, he is doing: $10 \times (2 \times G) = 20 \times G$.<br><br>

  Even though Alice and Bob never shared their private keys, they both ended up calculating the exact same thing: the 20th bounce from the starting point $G$.

  If you actually forced SageMath to calculate $20 \times G$ on our tiny Modulo 17 curve, the math spits out the coordinate (10, 6). Because multiplication works the same forwards and backwards, they are mathematically guaranteed to crash into the exact same point! That point is their shared secret, $S$.<br><br>

  <b>4. Encrypting the PIN</b><br>
  They both agree to use the X-coordinate of their shared secret (which is <b>10</b>) as their encryption key. <br>
  Alice takes her PIN (99) and XORs it with the shared secret (10).<br>
  $99 \oplus 10 = 105$<br><br>

  Alice sends the ciphertext <b>105</b> to Bob. Bob uses his copy of the shared secret (10) to XOR it back: $105 \oplus 10 = 99$. The PIN is securely transmitted!
</details>

<h2>The Problem</h2>

In the challenge, the author gave us $a = 2$, $b = 3$, and a Generator point $G = (5, 1)$. 

If you trust the author and plug those directly into SageMath, your script violently crashes with a massive traceback:

```text
Traceback (most recent call last):
  ...
  File "sage/schemes/elliptic_curves/ell_point.py", line 305, in __init__
    raise TypeError("Coordinates %s do not define a point on %s" % (list(v), curve))
TypeError: Coordinates [5, 1, 1] do not define a point on Elliptic Curve defined by y^2 = x^3 + 2*x + 3 over Finite Field of size 2305843009213693951
```

Why did SageMath crash? Let's do the math ourselves. We plug $x = 5$ into the author's equation: $y^2 = x^3 + 2x + 3$.
$$5^3 + 2(5) + 3$$
$$125 + 10 + 3 = 138$$

The right side equals **138**. But our $y$ is 1, and $1^2 = 1$. <br>
**1 does not equal 138!** The point $(5, 1)$ is physically not on the curve $y^2 = x^3 + 2x + 3$. The author lied to us about the parameters to intentionally break our script!

<h2>Recovering the Curve</h2>

We know points $G$ and $Q$ are valid points, we just don't know the *real* $A$ and $B$. Let's use high school algebra to find them.

Since both point $G(x_1, y_1)$ and point $Q(x_2, y_2)$ lie on the curve, we can set up a system of equations:
$$y_1^2 \equiv x_1^3 + Ax_1 + B \pmod p$$
$$y_2^2 \equiv x_2^3 + Ax_2 + B \pmod p$$

If we subtract the first equation from the second, the unknown $B$ cancels out entirely! We can then isolate $A$:
$$A \equiv \frac{(y_2^2 - y_1^2) - (x_2^3 - x_1^3)}{x_2 - x_1} \pmod p$$

Once we calculate $A$, finding $B$ is trivial. Here is how that looks in SageMath. Notice how we define our variables inside `GF(p)`. This tells SageMath to automatically apply the modulo to every single division and subtraction we do!

<div class="sage">
  <script type="text/x-sage">
p = 2305843009213693951
F = GF(p)

x1, y1 = F(5), F(1)
x2, y2 = F(1896662826602979990), F(1425560687073180440)
x3, y3 = F(320761691192926716), F(300507216662097367)

# Recovering A and B using algebra
A = (y2**2 - y1**2 - (x2**3 - x1**3)) / (x2 - x1)
B = y1**2 - x1**3 - A * x1

print(f"The REAL A: {A}")
print(f"The REAL B: {B}")

# Rebuilding the curve safely
E = EllipticCurve(F, [A, B])

G = E(x1, y1)
Q = E(x2, y2)
R = E(x3, y3)

# Crack the discrete log to steal the private key
print("\nSmashing the ECDLP...")
d_A = discrete_log(Q, G, operation='+')

# Calculate the shared secret
S = d_A * R
print(f"Shared Secret X-Coordinate: {S[0]}")

<h2>Decrypting the Payloads</h2>

Now we have the shared secret (`2199468938808738143`), but we still have two random ciphertexts. 

`cipher_1` is 20 bytes long.
`cipher_2` is 32 bytes long.

These lengths are a massive hint! 20 bytes is the exact digest size of **SHA-1**, and 32 bytes is the exact digest size of **SHA-256**. The CTF author took our shared secret $S$, hashed it, and used the hash as an XOR key for the ciphertexts.

Because we don't know if they hashed the raw bytes of the integer or the string representation of the integer, I wrote a Python script to test all possible materials and hash combinations automatically.

```python
import hashlib

S_x = 2199468938808738143
c1 = bytes.fromhex("dcb9e7887358f88e5ba5c366b459f93889ed77a6")
c2 = bytes.fromhex("eea0fb96656dc9ac0092d069fd5ec71998db4aad60764fedf3b75511ea34e151")

def long_to_bytes(val):
    return val.to_bytes((val.bit_length() + 7) // 8, 'big')

def xor(var, key):
    return bytes(a ^ b for a, b in zip(var, key))

# The author could have used raw bytes OR a string format, so we test both!
materials = [
    long_to_bytes(S_x),
    str(S_x).encode()
]

for mat in materials:
    hash_sha1 = hashlib.sha1(mat).digest()
    hash_sha256 = hashlib.sha256(mat).digest()
    hash_sha512 = hashlib.sha512(mat).digest()

    res1 = xor(c1, hash_sha1)
    res2 = xor(c2, hash_sha256)
    
    # Just in case they combined the ciphertexts and used SHA512
    res_combined = xor(c1 + c2, hash_sha512[:52])

    # Filter out garbage data. If the decrypted string is more than 70% 
    # readable ASCII characters, print it out!
    for res, name in [(res1, "c1 ^ SHA1"), (res2, "c2 ^ SHA256"), (res_combined, "c1+c2 ^ SHA512")]:
        if sum(1 for b in res if 32 <= b <= 126) > len(res) * 0.7:
            print(f"[{name}] -> {res}")
```

The script intelligently filters the outputs, throwing away any garbage XOR results and only printing text that actually looks like a flag.

<h1>Final Thoughts</h1>

This challenge is a great reminder that you don't always have to rely on the parameters given to you. Using high school algebra to rebuild $A$ and $B$ from the raw points is a bulletproof way to bypass tricky challenge descriptions. Once you have the curve, a tiny prime means the ECDLP falls apart, and the rest is just standard XOR manipulation. Really satisfying solve, hehe :3c
